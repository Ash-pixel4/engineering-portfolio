Print Quality Issues

A Comprehensive Guide to 40 Common Print Quality Issues

Introduction

This document serves as a technical guide for Customer Engineers, press operators, and quality control personnel engaged in sheet-fed and web offset lithography. It systematically addresses 40 prevalent print quality anomalies, providing a diagnostic framework for identification, an in-depth analysis of root causes, and actionable, field-level corrective measures. The objective is to facilitate efficient troubleshooting, minimize downtime, and ensure consistent adherence to print quality standards.

1. Backing Away / Low Flow

Definition: Backing away, also known as low flow, is a printing defect that occurs when the ink fails to transfer properly from the ink fountain to the fountain roller. The ink, despite being present in the fountain, does not flow sufficiently to replace the ink that is being transferred to the printing plate by the ductor roller. This results in a progressive starvation of ink to the printing unit.

Identification: The primary symptom of backing away is a gradual lightening of the printed image across the sheet or web as the press run continues. The print density becomes uneven and appears faded or washed out in certain areas. A visual inspection of the ink fountain will show that while there is ink in the fountain, it is not making proper contact with the fountain roller.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink is too short or buttery The ink’s rheology is incorrect, preventing it from flowing properly. An operator can modify the ink by adding an overprint varnish or other approved additives to increase its flow and length. Consulting the ink manufacturer for a formulation change is the long-term solution.
Insufficient ink in the fountain A low volume of ink in the fountain can lead to a loss of hydraulic pressure, preventing the ink from being forced against the fountain roller. The operator should add more ink to the fountain to ensure a consistent and adequate supply.
Lack of fountain agitation On presses not equipped with ink agitators, the ink can become stagnant and fail to flow. While not an immediate fix, the long-term solution is to retrofit the press with an ink agitation system. In the short term, the operator may need to manually agitate the ink.

2. Backtrapping / Contamination

Definition: Backtrapping, also referred to as color contamination, is a printing defect that occurs in multi-color printing when ink from a previously printed unit transfers back to the blankets of subsequent printing units. This leads to the contamination of the ink colors in the fountains of the following units, resulting in color shifts and inconsistencies in the printed output.

Identification: The most evident sign of backtrapping is a visible change in the hue of the printed colors. For example, if black ink from the first unit contaminates the yellow ink in the second unit, the yellow will appear muddy or greenish. This contamination is often visible on the printing blanket and rollers of the affected unit.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect ink tack sequence In wet-on-wet printing, the inks should be printed in a sequence of decreasing tack. If a subsequent ink has a higher tack than the previous one, it will pull the previous ink off the substrate and onto its own blanket. The operator should verify that the inks are being used in the correct tack sequence.
Excessive ink film thickness A thick film of ink is more likely to transfer back to subsequent blankets. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.
Fountain solution issues An incorrect pH or conductivity of the fountain solution can affect the ink’s ability to set properly on the substrate, making it more susceptible to backtrapping. The operator should check and adjust the fountain solution to the manufacturer’s specifications.

3. Dot Gain

Definition: Dot gain, also known as Tone Value Increase (TVI), is a phenomenon in which the size of halftone dots increases from the printing plate to the final printed sheet. This results in a darker, muddier appearance than intended. It is a natural and unavoidable part of the printing process, but it must be controlled and compensated for to achieve accurate color reproduction.

Identification: Dot gain is identified by measuring the dot area of a printed halftone tint with a densitometer or spectrophotometer and comparing it to the original value on the printing plate. Visually, excessive dot gain is apparent as a loss of detail in shadow areas, a darkening of mid-tones, and a general lack of sharpness in the printed image.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Excessive printing pressure Too much pressure between the plate and blanket, or between the blanket and substrate, will squash the halftone dots and cause them to enlarge. The operator should check and adjust the printing pressures to the minimum required for a clean transfer of ink.
Incorrect ink/water balance An excessive amount of fountain solution can cause the ink to spread, leading to dot gain. The operator should maintain a proper ink/water balance, using the minimum amount of water necessary to keep the non-image areas of the plate clean.
Paper characteristics The absorbency and surface characteristics of the paper have a significant impact on dot gain. Uncoated papers, for example, exhibit more dot gain than coated papers. While the operator cannot change the paper, they can adjust other press settings to compensate.

4. Ghosting (Mechanical)

Definition: Mechanical ghosting is a printing defect that appears as a faint, unwanted image or pattern in the printed area. It is caused by issues with the inking system of the press, where the rollers are unable to re-charge with a sufficient amount of ink in time to evenly ink the entire plate cylinder. This is distinct from chemical ghosting, which is related to drying and chemical reactions.

Identification: Mechanical ghosting is typically visible as a lighter or darker area that mirrors the shape of a previously printed element on the same sheet. It is most noticeable in large solid areas or heavy tones. The ghosted image will be in the same position on every sheet of the run.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect roller settings Improperly set form rollers can lead to uneven ink distribution on the plate. The operator should check and adjust the form roller settings to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Worn or incorrect diameter rollers Rollers that are worn or have an incorrect diameter will not transfer ink evenly. The operator should inspect the rollers for wear and replace them if necessary.
Poor job layout A layout that has a heavy ink coverage area immediately followed by a light coverage area can starve the inking system. While not always possible to change, the operator can sometimes add a “take-off bar” to the edge of the sheet to help consume excess ink and even out the ink distribution.

5. Hickeys

Definition: A hickey is a printing defect that appears as a small, unwanted spot or speck on the printed sheet, often surrounded by a white or unprinted “halo.” Hickeys are caused by foreign particles that adhere to the printing plate or blanket, preventing the ink from transferring to the paper in that specific spot.

Identification: Hickeys are easily identifiable as small, sharp-edged specks in the printed area. The halo effect around the speck is a key characteristic. The particle causing the hickey can sometimes be found on the printing blanket. The shape of the hickey can sometimes indicate its source; for example, a fiber-shaped hickey is likely from paper lint, while a round hickey may be from a dried ink particle.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Dried ink particles Small particles of dried ink can flake off the rollers or from the ink fountain and cause hickeys. The operator should maintain good press hygiene, cleaning the rollers and fountain regularly.
Paper lint or dust Loose fibers or dust from the paper can adhere to the blanket. The operator can fan the paper before loading it into the press and wipe the edges of the pile with a tack rag.
Worn rollers Worn or damaged rollers can shed small particles of rubber. The operator should inspect the rollers for signs of wear and replace them as needed.

6. Linting and Picking

Definition: Linting and picking are related printing defects that involve the pulling of fibers or coating from the paper surface during the printing process. Linting refers to the accumulation of loose paper fibers on the printing blanket, while picking is a more severe form where larger pieces of the paper’s surface or coating are lifted away, leaving a void in the printed image.

Identification: Linting is identified by a gradual build-up of fibers on the blanket, which can lead to a fuzzy or unsharp appearance in the printed image. Picking is more obvious, appearing as unprinted spots or voids in the printed area, often with the lifted paper fragment visible on the blanket.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Tacky ink Ink with excessive tack will exert a strong pulling force on the paper surface. The operator can reduce the ink’s tack by adding a tack reducer or by consulting the ink manufacturer for a less tacky formulation.
Weak paper surface The paper itself may have a low surface strength, making it susceptible to linting and picking. While the operator cannot change the paper, they can try to reduce the printing pressure and ink tack to minimize the stress on the paper.
Excessive printing pressure Too much pressure between the blanket and the paper can increase the force exerted on the paper surface. The operator should reduce the printing pressure to the minimum required for good ink transfer.

7. Metering Roller Build-up

Definition: Metering roller build-up is the accumulation of ink, paper dust, or other debris on the metering roller of the dampening system. This build-up interferes with the roller’s ability to meter a consistent and even film of fountain solution to the printing plate, leading to various ink and water balance problems.

Identification: A visual inspection of the metering roller will reveal a visible accumulation of residue. This build-up can be uneven and may have a glazed or shiny appearance. The print quality will be inconsistent, with symptoms such as scumming, tinting, or emulsification appearing intermittently.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Contaminated dampening solution If the dampening solution is contaminated with ink, paper particles, or other debris, this contamination can transfer to the metering roller. The operator should regularly clean and replace the dampening solution.
Poorly maintained rollers Dirty or worn rollers in the dampening system can contribute to the build-up on the metering roller. The operator should follow a regular roller cleaning and maintenance schedule.
Linty paper Paper that sheds a lot of lint or dust can contaminate the entire printing system, including the dampening rollers. While the operator cannot change the paper, they can ensure that the press’s cleaning systems are functioning correctly.

8. Misting or Slinging

Definition: Ink misting, also known as ink slinging or flying, is a defect where the ink breaks into fine droplets and is thrown from the rapidly rotating rollers of the press by centrifugal force. This creates a fine spray of ink that can contaminate the press, the surrounding environment, and even the printed sheets.

Identification: The most obvious sign of misting is a visible cloud or spray of ink around the printing unit. A fine layer of ink will also be found on the press guards, floor, and other surfaces. The printed sheets may have a slightly hazy or speckled appearance.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink is too long or stringy Inks with a long, stringy rheology are more prone to misting. The operator can try adding a shortener to the ink, but the best solution is to work with the ink manufacturer to obtain a formulation with the correct properties for the press and running conditions.
Excessive press speed The faster the press runs, the greater the centrifugal force on the ink. The operator should run the press at the manufacturer’s recommended speed for the ink and substrate being used.
Improper roller settings Incorrectly set rollers can cause excessive ink shearing and agitation, leading to misting. The operator should check and adjust all roller settings to the manufacturer’s specifications.

9. Mottling

Definition: Mottling is a printing defect characterized by an uneven, blotchy, or cloudy appearance in solid printed areas. It is a non-uniformity in print density that can be caused by a variety of factors related to the ink, paper, and press conditions.

Identification: Mottling is identified by a visual inspection of solid printed areas. Instead of a smooth, uniform color, the area will have a speckled, grainy, or blotchy appearance. The mottle can be random or may form a pattern.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Poor ink transfer If the ink does not transfer smoothly from the blanket to the paper, it can result in mottling. The operator should check and adjust the printing pressure and ensure that the blankets are in good condition.
Uneven paper surface or absorption The surface characteristics of the paper play a crucial role in print uniformity. A paper with an uneven surface or variable absorbency will cause the ink to be absorbed unevenly, leading to mottling. The operator can try a different paper stock if this is a recurring problem.
Incorrect ink and water balance An improper ink and water balance can lead to a variety of print defects, including mottling. The operator should adjust the ink and water settings to achieve a stable balance.

10. pH / Conductivity Varies Widely

Definition: This refers to significant and uncontrolled fluctuations in the pH and conductivity of the fountain solution during a press run. The pH and conductivity of the fountain solution are critical parameters that affect the ink and water balance, plate life, and overall print quality. Instability in these parameters leads to inconsistent and unpredictable printing conditions.

Identification: This issue is not identified by a specific visual defect, but rather by a range of inconsistent print problems, such as scumming, tinting, plate wear, and color variations. The only way to definitively identify this problem is to regularly measure the pH and conductivity of the fountain solution and observe significant variations over time.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Contamination of the fountain solution The fountain solution can become contaminated with ink, paper dust, and other foreign matter during the press run, which will alter its pH and conductivity. The operator should regularly clean and replace the fountain solution.
Incorrect mixing of the fountain solution concentrate If the fountain solution concentrate is not mixed with water in the correct ratio, the pH and conductivity will be incorrect from the start. The operator must follow the manufacturer’s instructions carefully when mixing the solution.
Poor quality water The quality of the water used to mix the fountain solution is critical. Water with a high mineral content or other impurities can cause the pH and conductivity to be unstable. The use of a water filtration or treatment system is recommended.

11. Piling

Definition: Piling is the accumulation of ink, paper coating, or fibers on the printing blanket, plate, or rollers. This build-up interferes with the transfer of ink and can cause a variety of print defects, including voids in the printed image, spots, and streaks.

Identification: Piling is identified by a visual inspection of the press components. A build-up of material will be visible on the blankets, plates, or rollers. The printed sheets will show corresponding defects, such as unprinted areas or spots.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink is too tacky A highly tacky ink will pull fibers and coating from the paper surface, leading to piling. The operator can reduce the ink’s tack by adding a tack reducer.
Weak paper surface Paper with a low surface strength is more prone to shedding fibers and coating. If piling is a persistent problem, the operator may need to suggest a different paper stock.
Incorrect ink/water balance An improper ink and water balance can contribute to piling. The operator should adjust the ink and water settings to achieve a stable balance.

12. Plate Blinding or Wear

Definition: Plate blinding, also known as plate wear, is the loss of ink receptivity in the image areas of the printing plate. The image areas become desensitized and fail to accept or transfer ink properly, resulting in a weak or missing print. This is a progressive issue that worsens as the press run continues.

Identification: The most obvious sign of plate blinding is a gradual fading or disappearance of the printed image in certain areas. The affected areas will appear weak, washed out, or may not print at all. A visual inspection of the printing plate will show that the image area has lost its ink-receptive properties.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Aggressive fountain solution A fountain solution with a pH that is too low or a conductivity that is too high can chemically attack the image area of the plate, causing it to become desensitized. The operator should check and adjust the fountain solution to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Abrasive pigments in ink Some inks contain abrasive pigments that can cause excessive wear on the printing plate. If plate wear is a recurring problem, the operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to see if a less abrasive ink formulation is available.
Excessive printing pressure Too much pressure between the plate and blanket can cause physical wear on the plate. The operator should check and adjust the printing pressures to the minimum required for a clean transfer of ink.

13. Poor Scuff Resistance

Definition: Poor scuff resistance is a defect where the printed ink film is easily scratched, rubbed, or marked during finishing operations or by normal handling. The ink does not adhere well to the substrate and is not durable enough to withstand the intended use of the printed product.

Identification: This defect is identified by subjecting the printed sheet to a scuff or rub test. If the ink easily transfers, scratches, or shows signs of abrasion, it has poor scuff resistance. This is often discovered during post-press operations such as cutting, folding, or binding.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink not fully dried or cured If the ink is not completely dry, it will have very poor scuff resistance. The operator should ensure that the press’s drying system is functioning correctly and that the ink is given sufficient time to dry before handling.
Incorrect ink formulation The ink itself may not have the necessary properties for good scuff resistance. The operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to ensure that the correct ink is being used for the substrate and the intended application. The addition of a scuff-resistant varnish or coating can also be considered.
Excessive use of spray powder While spray powder is used to prevent set-off, an excessive amount can interfere with the ink’s ability to form a tough, durable film. The operator should use the minimum amount of spray powder necessary.

14. Poor Transfer of Ink

Definition: Poor transfer of ink is a general term for a condition where the ink does not transfer efficiently and completely from the printing plate to the blanket, and from the blanket to the substrate. This results in a weak, uneven, or incomplete printed image.

Identification: The printed image will appear light, washed out, or may have missing areas. The print density will be low, even if the ink fountain is full and the inking rollers are fully charged. A visual inspection of the blanket may show that the ink is not transferring cleanly from the plate.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect printing pressure Insufficient pressure between the plate and blanket, or between the blanket and substrate, will result in poor ink transfer. The operator should check and adjust the printing pressures to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Glazed or worn blankets and rollers Blankets and rollers that have become glazed (hard and shiny) or worn will not transfer ink properly. The operator should follow a regular maintenance schedule for cleaning and deglazing blankets and rollers, and replace them when they become worn.
Incorrect ink/water balance An excessive amount of fountain solution on the plate can interfere with ink transfer. The operator should maintain a proper ink/water balance, using the minimum amount of water necessary.

15. Poor Trapping - Process Inks

Definition: Poor trapping is a defect in multi-color process printing where a subsequent ink does not adhere properly to a previously printed ink. This results in incorrect secondary and tertiary colors (e.g., greens, purples, and browns) and a muddy or washed-out appearance in the final printed image.

Identification: Poor trapping is identified by measuring the densities of the overprinted colors and comparing them to the densities of the individual inks. A significant loss of density in the overprinted areas indicates poor trapping. Visually, the colors will appear weak and incorrect.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect ink tack sequence In wet-on-wet printing, the inks must be printed in a sequence of decreasing tack. If a subsequent ink has a higher tack than the previous one, it will not transfer properly. The operator must ensure that the inks are being used in the correct tack sequence (typically K, C, M, Y).
Excessive ink film thickness A thick film of the first-down ink can prevent the subsequent inks from adhering properly. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness of all colors to the minimum required for the desired density.
Fountain solution issues An incorrect pH or conductivity of the fountain solution can affect the ink’s ability to set on the substrate, leading to poor trapping. The operator should check and adjust the fountain solution to the manufacturer’s specifications.

16. Slow Drying and Slow Setting

Definition: Slow drying and slow setting are related defects where the ink does not dry or set on the substrate within the expected timeframe. Slow setting refers to the initial phase where the ink vehicle is absorbed into the paper, while slow drying refers to the final phase where the ink hardens through oxidation and polymerization. Both result in an ink film that is wet or soft to the touch, leading to problems such as set-off and poor scuff resistance.

Identification: The most obvious sign is that the ink on the printed sheets is still wet or easily smudged long after it has been printed. This can be checked by a simple rub test. The sheets may also stick together in the delivery pile.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect ink formulation The ink may not be formulated correctly for the substrate or the press conditions. The operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to ensure that the correct ink and driers are being used.
Excessive ink film thickness A thick film of ink will take longer to dry. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.
Low temperature or high humidity Environmental conditions have a significant impact on ink drying. Low temperatures and high humidity will slow down the drying process. The operator should ensure that the pressroom is maintained at a suitable temperature and humidity.

17. Slurring

Definition: Slurring is a printing defect characterized by the smearing or elongation of halftone dots or type in the direction of paper travel. It is caused by slippage between the paper and the blanket, or between the blanket and the plate, during the impression. This is different from doubling, which is a double image.

Identification: Slurring is identified by a visual inspection of the printed sheet. The dots will appear elongated or smeared, and the trailing edges of printed elements will be fuzzy or indistinct. It is most noticeable in areas of fine detail or small type.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect packing Over-packing of the plate or blanket cylinder can cause a difference in surface speed, leading to slippage. The operator should check and adjust the packing to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Loose blanket A blanket that is not properly tensioned can move or slip during the impression. The operator should ensure that the blanket is tightened to the correct torque.
Worn cylinder bearers Worn bearers on the plate or blanket cylinders can cause inconsistent pressure and lead to slippage. The operator should inspect the bearers for wear and have them replaced if necessary.

18. Streaks in Print (Non-Gear Streaks)

Definition: Non-gear streaks are continuous lines or bands of lighter or darker print that appear across the printed sheet, in the direction of paper travel. Unlike gear streaks, they do not correspond to the gear teeth of the press. They are caused by a variety of issues related to the inking and dampening systems.

Identification: Non-gear streaks are identified as continuous lines or bands running parallel to the direction of paper travel. They can be sharp or fuzzy, and may be intermittent or continuous throughout the run.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect roller settings Improperly set inking or dampening rollers can cause uneven pressure and lead to streaks. The operator should check and adjust all roller settings to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Worn or damaged rollers Rollers that are worn, glazed, or damaged will not transfer ink or water evenly. The operator should inspect the rollers and replace them if they are not in good condition.
Dried ink or debris on rollers A build-up of dried ink or other debris on the rollers can cause streaks. The operator should maintain a regular roller cleaning schedule.

19. Stripping

Definition: Ink stripping is a condition where the ink rollers, particularly the form rollers, lose their affinity for ink and become wet with fountain solution. This prevents the rollers from carrying and transferring ink properly, resulting in a loss of print density or a complete failure to print.

Identification: The most obvious sign of stripping is a loss of print density in the corresponding area of the sheet. A visual inspection of the ink rollers will show that they are wet with fountain solution and are not carrying a uniform film of ink.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Aggressive fountain solution A fountain solution that is too acidic or has too high a concentration of etch can chemically sensitize the rollers to water. The operator should check and adjust the fountain solution to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Glazed rollers Rollers that have become glazed (hard and shiny) are more susceptible to stripping. The operator should deglaze the rollers as part of a regular maintenance program.
Contamination Contamination of the ink or fountain solution with foreign substances can lead to stripping. The operator should ensure that all materials are clean and free from contamination.

20. Tailing

Definition: Tailing is a printing defect that appears as a streak or smear of ink extending from the trailing edge of a printed element, typically a solid or a heavy tone. It is caused by an excess of ink that is not fully transferred to the substrate and is then carried along by the motion of the press.

Identification: Tailing is identified by a visible streak of ink that appears to be dragged from the trailing edge of a printed area. It is most prominent in solids and reverses.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink is too fluid An ink that is too low in viscosity can be prone to tailing. The operator can try to modify the ink with an additive to increase its body, but the best solution is to consult with the ink manufacturer.
Excessive ink film thickness A heavy film of ink is more likely to be pulled from the printed area. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.
Incorrect roller settings Improperly set rollers can lead to an uneven ink film and contribute to tailing. The operator should check and adjust all roller settings to the manufacturer’s specifications.

21. Tinting / Toning / Scumming

Definition: Tinting, toning, and scumming are related defects where the non-image areas of the printing plate pick up ink, resulting in a dirty or discolored background on the printed sheet. Scumming is a more severe and localized form, often caused by a desensitized plate. Tinting is a uniform, light discoloration of the background, usually caused by ink emulsifying in the fountain solution. Toning is similar to tinting but is often more pronounced.

Identification: These defects are identified by the presence of ink in the non-image areas of the printed sheet. The background will appear dirty, hazy, or discolored. Scumming will appear as distinct, localized areas of ink, while tinting will be a more uniform, overall discoloration.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect ink/water balance Too much ink or not enough fountain solution will cause the non-image areas of the plate to become ink-receptive. The operator must maintain a proper ink/water balance.
Fountain solution is too weak or contaminated A fountain solution that is not mixed correctly or has become contaminated will not be effective at keeping the non-image areas clean. The operator should check the pH and conductivity of the fountain solution and replace it if necessary.
Plate is not properly desensitized If the plate is not properly processed or has become scratched or worn, the non-image areas can lose their water-receptive properties. The operator should inspect the plate and replace it if it is damaged.

22. Set-Off

Definition: Set-off is the unwanted transfer of ink from a freshly printed sheet to the back of the sheet that is delivered on top of it in the delivery pile. This is a common problem that occurs when the ink is not sufficiently dry or set before the sheets are stacked.

Identification: Set-off is identified by a faint, mirror image of the printed content on the back of the adjacent sheet. It is most noticeable in areas of heavy ink coverage.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink drying too slowly If the ink does not set or dry quickly enough, it will still be wet when the next sheet is delivered on top of it. The operator should ensure that the press’s drying system is functioning correctly and that the appropriate driers are being used in the ink.
Excessive ink film thickness A heavy film of ink will take longer to dry and is more likely to cause set-off. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.
Improper use of spray powder An insufficient amount of spray powder, or a powder with the wrong particle size, will not provide enough separation between the sheets in the delivery pile. The operator should adjust the spray powder unit to apply a uniform, light coating of the correct powder.

23. Vertical Scratch

Definition: A vertical scratch is a fine, sharp line that appears on the printed sheet, running in the direction of paper travel. It is caused by a sharp object or particle that is scratching the printing plate or blanket.

Identification: A vertical scratch is identified as a continuous, fine line in the printed image. The scratch will appear in the same position on every sheet. A careful inspection of the printing plate and blanket may reveal a corresponding scratch or score mark.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Debris embedded in a roller or blanket A small, hard particle, such as a piece of sand or metal, can become embedded in a roller or blanket and cause a continuous scratch on the plate. The operator should carefully clean and inspect all rollers and blankets.
Damaged doctor blade In a gravure or flexographic press, a damaged or nicked doctor blade can cause a fine scratch in the printed image. The operator should inspect the doctor blade and replace it if it is damaged.
Foreign object in the press A loose screw, piece of metal, or other foreign object can fall into the press and cause a scratch. The operator should perform a thorough inspection of the press to locate and remove any foreign objects.

24. Fog (Fogging)

Definition: Fogging, also known as snowflaking, is a printing defect that appears as a random, speckled pattern of unprinted dots in a solid or heavily toned area. It is caused by a failure of the ink to completely wet the surface of the substrate, leaving microscopic voids in the ink film.

Identification: Fogging is identified by a visual inspection of solid printed areas. The area will have a speckled, textured appearance, as if it has been lightly dusted with snow. The effect is often subtle and may require magnification to see clearly.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Poor ink transfer If the ink does not transfer properly from the blanket to the substrate, it can result in fogging. The operator should check and adjust the printing pressure and ensure that the blankets are in good condition.
Substrate issues The surface energy and texture of the substrate can affect the ink’s ability to wet the surface. If fogging is a persistent problem, the operator may need to try a different substrate.
Incorrect ink formulation The ink may not have the correct surface tension or other properties to properly wet the substrate. The operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to obtain a more suitable ink.

25. Density Variations

Definition: Density variations are fluctuations in the optical density of the printed ink film, both across a single sheet and from sheet to sheet during a press run. This results in an inconsistent and uneven appearance in the printed product.

Identification: Density variations are identified by measuring the print density with a densitometer at various points on the sheet and at various times during the run. Visually, the print will appear lighter or darker in some areas than in others.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Inconsistent ink feed If the ink is not fed consistently from the fountain to the inking rollers, the ink film thickness will vary. The operator should check the ink fountain settings and ensure that the ink is flowing properly.
Fluctuations in press speed Changes in press speed can affect the ink and water balance and lead to density variations. The operator should maintain a consistent press speed throughout the run.
Temperature changes The temperature of the press and the ink can affect the ink’s viscosity and flow properties. The operator should ensure that the pressroom is maintained at a stable temperature.

26. Gear Marks

Definition: Gear marks are a printing defect that appears as a series of light and dark bands or streaks across the printed sheet, perpendicular to the direction of paper travel. These streaks correspond to the meshing of the gears in the press, and are caused by vibrations or inconsistencies in the gear train.

Identification: Gear marks are identified as a repeating pattern of streaks that are evenly spaced and run across the width of the sheet. The spacing of the streaks will match the pitch of the press gears. They are most noticeable in solid or heavily toned areas.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Worn or damaged gears Worn or damaged gears in the press drive train can cause vibrations and lead to gear marks. The operator should have the press’s gear train inspected by a qualified mechanic and have any worn or damaged gears replaced.
Improper lubrication Insufficient or improper lubrication of the gears can cause them to run rough and create vibrations. The operator should follow the manufacturer’s recommended lubrication schedule.
Incorrect backlash Backlash is the clearance between the teeth of meshing gears. If the backlash is incorrect, it can cause the gears to chatter and create vibrations. The adjustment of gear backlash is a job for a qualified press mechanic.

27. Color Variations

Definition: Color variations are noticeable and unacceptable differences in color, either across a single printed sheet or between different sheets in the same press run. This results in a lack of consistency and a failure to match the approved color proof.

Identification: Color variations are identified by comparing the printed sheets to the color proof or a known standard. A spectrophotometer can be used to measure the color and quantify the variation. Visually, the colors will appear to be off-shade, either lighter, darker, or shifted in hue.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Inconsistent ink film thickness Variations in the thickness of the ink film will cause the color to appear lighter or darker. The operator must maintain a consistent ink film thickness by carefully controlling the ink fountain settings.
Fluctuations in ink and water balance Changes in the ink and water balance can affect the color of the ink. The operator must maintain a stable ink and water balance throughout the run.
Changes in press speed Variations in press speed can affect the ink and water balance and lead to color variations. The operator should maintain a consistent press speed.

28. Misregistration

Definition: Misregistration is a printing defect where the different colors of a multi-color print do not align correctly with each other. This results in a fuzzy, out-of-focus appearance and can cause color shifts at the edges of objects.

Identification: Misregistration is identified by a visual inspection of the printed sheet. The different colors will not be perfectly aligned, and there will be visible gaps or overlaps between them. Registration marks, which are small crosshair targets printed in the trim area of the sheet, are used to check for and adjust registration.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Plate mounting errors If the printing plates are not mounted correctly on the plate cylinders, they will not be in register with each other. The operator must take care to mount the plates accurately.
Paper instability Paper can stretch or shrink as it passes through the press, especially if it is exposed to changes in humidity. This can cause the registration to vary from the front to the back of the sheet. The operator should ensure that the paper is properly conditioned to the pressroom environment before printing.
Mechanical press issues Worn or loose components in the press, such as cylinder bearings or grippers, can cause the registration to be inconsistent. The operator should have the press inspected by a mechanic if registration problems persist.

29. Smudging

Definition: Smudging is a printing defect where the ink is smeared or rubbed after it has been printed, resulting in a loss of sharpness and detail. This is different from slurring, which occurs during the impression. Smudging happens after the impression, usually due to improper handling of the wet sheets.

Identification: Smudging is identified by a smeared or blurry appearance in the printed image. The direction of the smudge is often random and is not necessarily in the direction of paper travel. It is most noticeable in areas of heavy ink coverage.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink drying too slowly If the ink is still wet when the sheets are handled, it will be easily smudged. The operator should ensure that the press’s drying system is functioning correctly and that the ink is given sufficient time to dry.
Improper handling of printed sheets Rough handling of the printed sheets before the ink is fully dry will cause smudging. The operator should handle the sheets carefully and avoid rubbing or sliding them against each other.
Excessive set-off If there is excessive set-off in the delivery pile, the movement of the sheets against each other can cause smudging. The operator should use an appropriate amount of spray powder to prevent set-off.

30. Blanket Contamination

Definition: Blanket contamination is the accumulation of foreign matter, such as paper dust, lint, or dried ink particles, on the surface of the printing blanket. This contamination interferes with the transfer of ink and can cause a variety of print defects, including hickeys, spots, and voids.

Identification: Blanket contamination is identified by a visual inspection of the printing blanket. The surface of the blanket will have visible particles or a build-up of residue. The printed sheets will show corresponding defects.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Linty or dusty paper Paper that sheds a lot of lint or dust is a common source of blanket contamination. The operator can fan the paper before loading it and ensure that the press’s cleaning systems are working effectively.
Poor press hygiene A dirty pressroom environment can contribute to blanket contamination. The operator should maintain good housekeeping practices and keep the press and surrounding area clean.
Dried ink particles Particles of dried ink can flake off the rollers and contaminate the blanket. The operator should follow a regular roller cleaning schedule to prevent the build-up of dried ink.

31. Ink Emulsification

Definition: Ink emulsification is a condition where the ink absorbs an excessive amount of fountain solution, forming a water-in-ink emulsion. A certain amount of emulsification is necessary for the lithographic process, but when it becomes excessive, it can lead to a variety of print problems, including weak colors, poor drying, and a loss of print quality.

Identification: Excessively emulsified ink will appear weak, soupy, and may have a greasy or shiny appearance on the rollers. The printed image will be low in density and may have a washed-out look. The ink may also take an unusually long time to dry.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Fountain solution is too aggressive A fountain solution with a pH that is too low or a conductivity that is too high can cause the ink to break down and absorb too much water. The operator should check and adjust the fountain solution to the manufacturer’s specifications.
Ink is not formulated for the press conditions Some inks are more prone to emulsification than others. The operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to ensure that the ink is suitable for the press and the fountain solution being used.
Excessive water feed Running too much water on the press is a common cause of ink emulsification. The operator should reduce the water feed to the minimum amount necessary to keep the non-image areas of the plate clean.

32. Water Marks

Definition: Water marks, also known as water spots or drips, are a printing defect that appears as irregular, blotchy areas in the printed image. They are caused by an excess of fountain solution on the printing plate, which prevents the ink from adhering properly.

Identification: Water marks are identified as randomly shaped, light or unprinted areas in the printed image. They can be small spots or large, irregular blotches. They are often caused by drips or splashes of fountain solution on the plate.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Excessive dampening Running too much fountain solution on the press is the most common cause of water marks. The operator should reduce the dampening system settings to the minimum required to keep the plate clean.
Leaky dampening system A leak in the dampening system can cause drips of fountain solution to fall onto the plate. The operator should inspect the dampening system for leaks and make any necessary repairs.
Condensation If the pressroom is very humid, condensation can form on the cold surfaces of the press, such as the chill rollers, and drip onto the plate. The operator should ensure that the pressroom is maintained at a suitable humidity level.

33. Chalking

Definition: Chalking is a printing defect where the ink pigment does not bind properly to the substrate and can be easily rubbed off as a dry, powdery substance, similar to chalk. This occurs when the ink vehicle is absorbed too rapidly into the paper, leaving the pigment behind on the surface without a binder to hold it in place.

Identification: Chalking is identified by rubbing the printed surface with a finger or a cloth. If a powdery residue of pigment is easily removed, the ink is chalking. The printed image will also have a dull, lifeless appearance.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Highly absorbent paper Paper that is very absorbent can wick the ink vehicle away from the pigment before the ink has had a chance to set and dry properly. The operator can try adding a binder or varnish to the ink to help hold the pigment in place.
Incorrect ink formulation The ink may not have the correct balance of vehicle and pigment for the paper being used. The operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to obtain a more suitable ink.
Fountain solution issues An overly acidic fountain solution can interfere with the ink’s drying and binding properties. The operator should check and adjust the pH of the fountain solution.

34. Bronzing

Definition: Bronzing is a printing defect that appears as a metallic, bronze-like sheen on the surface of a printed ink film, particularly in dark, heavily inked areas. It is an optical effect caused by the reflection of light from the fine particles of pigment that are not properly wetted by the ink vehicle.

Identification: Bronzing is identified by a metallic or iridescent sheen that is visible when the printed sheet is viewed at an angle. It is most common with blue and black inks. The effect is often more pronounced in the shadow areas of the image.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Incorrect ink formulation Some pigments are more prone to bronzing than others. The operator should consult with the ink manufacturer to see if a less prone ink is available.
Excessive ink film thickness A heavy film of ink is more likely to exhibit bronzing. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.
Slow ink setting If the ink sets too slowly, the pigment can rise to the surface of the ink film and cause bronzing. The operator can try adding a small amount of drier to the ink to speed up the setting time.

35. Show-Through

Definition: Show-through is a printing defect where the printed image on one side of a sheet is visible from the reverse side. This is not to be confused with strike-through, where the ink actually penetrates through the paper. Show-through is a result of the paper’s lack of opacity.

Identification: Show-through is identified by viewing the unprinted side of a sheet and seeing a faint image of the print from the other side. The severity of show-through depends on the opacity of the paper and the density of the print.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Low paper opacity The primary cause of show-through is the use of a paper with insufficient opacity for the job. While the operator cannot change the paper, they can try to minimize the print density to reduce the severity of the show-through.
Excessive ink film thickness A heavy film of ink is more likely to be visible from the reverse side of the sheet. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.

36. Strike-Through

Definition: Strike-through is a printing defect where the ink penetrates completely through the paper, becoming visible on the reverse side. This is different from show-through, which is a result of the paper’s lack of opacity. Strike-through is caused by an overly absorbent paper or an ink with a very low viscosity.

Identification: Strike-through is identified by the presence of ink stains on the back of the printed sheet that correspond to the printed image on the front. The ink will have a fuzzy, feathered appearance where it has bled through the paper.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Highly absorbent paper Paper that is very porous and absorbent will allow the ink to penetrate through it. If strike-through is a problem, a less absorbent paper stock should be considered for future jobs.
Ink is too fluid An ink with a very low viscosity will be more likely to penetrate through the paper. The operator can try adding a bodying gum or other additive to the ink to increase its viscosity.

37. Doubling

Definition: Doubling is a printing defect that appears as a double image, where a second, lighter impression of the printed element appears slightly offset from the main image. This is different from slurring, which is a smearing of the image. Doubling is caused by an unwanted transfer of ink.

Identification: Doubling is identified by a distinct double image, with a lighter, ghost-like image appearing next to the main printed element. It is most noticeable in areas of fine detail or small type.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Loose blanket A blanket that is not properly tensioned can move or slip during the impression, causing a double image. The operator should ensure that the blanket is tightened to the correct torque.
Worn cylinder gears Worn or damaged gears in the cylinder drive train can cause a slight bounce or hesitation during the impression, leading to a double image. This is a mechanical issue that requires the attention of a press mechanic.
Paper feed issues If the paper is not held securely by the grippers as it passes through the press, it can shift during the impression and cause a double image. The operator should check the gripper settings and ensure that they are holding the paper firmly.

38. Fill-In

Definition: Fill-in, also known as plugging, is a printing defect where the spaces in halftone dots or fine reverse type become filled with ink. This results in a loss of detail and a dark, muddy appearance in the printed image.

Identification: Fill-in is identified by a visual inspection of the printed sheet. The fine details in the image will be lost, and the shadow areas will appear dark and plugged. Reverse type will be filled in and unreadable.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Ink is too fluid An ink with a very low viscosity can be prone to filling in fine details. The operator can try adding a bodying gum to the ink to increase its viscosity.
Excessive ink film thickness A heavy film of ink is more likely to fill in fine details. The operator should reduce the ink film thickness to the minimum required for the desired density.
Linty paper Lint and dust from the paper can accumulate on the blanket and contribute to fill-in. The operator should use a less linty paper if possible and ensure that the press’s cleaning systems are working effectively.

39. Web Breaks

Definition: A web break is a catastrophic event in web-fed printing where the continuous roll of paper (the web) tears or breaks while the press is running. This results in significant downtime for the press, as the web must be re-threaded through the entire press.

Identification: A web break is immediately obvious, as the press will automatically shut down and an alarm will sound. The paper will be torn, and the press will be in a state of disarray.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Defects in the paper roll The paper roll may have a variety of defects, such as a splice, a tear, or a hole, that can cause a web break. The operator should inspect the paper roll for defects before loading it into the press.
Incorrect web tension The tension of the web as it passes through the press is critical. If the tension is too high, the paper can be stretched to the breaking point. If the tension is too low, the paper can become slack and get caught in the rollers. The operator must carefully set and monitor the web tension.
Mechanical issues A variety of mechanical issues, such as a misaligned roller or a sharp edge on a press component, can cause a web break. The operator should perform regular inspections of the press to identify and correct any potential problems.

40. Fluting

Definition: Fluting is a printing defect that appears as a series of parallel, wavy lines or ridges in the printed image, running in the direction of paper travel. It is a characteristic problem of web-fed printing and is caused by the uneven drying of the paper as it passes through the heatset oven.

Identification: Fluting is identified by a visual inspection of the printed sheet. The surface of the paper will have a wavy or corrugated appearance, and the printed image will be distorted. The flutes are typically evenly spaced and run the length of the sheet.

Causes and Solutions:

Cause Operator-Level Solution
Excessive heat in the oven If the oven temperature is too high, the moisture in the paper will be evaporated too quickly, causing the paper to shrink unevenly and form flutes. The operator should reduce the oven temperature to the minimum required to dry the ink.
Incorrect web tension If the web tension is too low as the paper enters the oven, it can be more susceptible to fluting. The operator should ensure that the web tension is set correctly.
Paper characteristics Some papers are more prone to fluting than others, depending on their fiber composition and moisture content. If fluting is a persistent problem, a different paper stock may need to be considered.

Conclusion

The 40 print quality issues detailed in this document represent the most common challenges faced in the sheet-fed and web offset printing industries. A thorough understanding of these issues, their causes, and the methods for identifying and resolving them is essential for any printing professional. By implementing the operator-level solutions and preventive measures outlined in this guide, printing companies can significantly improve their print quality, reduce waste, and increase overall efficiency. Continuous training, a commitment to quality control, and a collaborative relationship with ink and paper suppliers are key to achieving consistent, high-quality results in the dynamic and demanding world of print.
